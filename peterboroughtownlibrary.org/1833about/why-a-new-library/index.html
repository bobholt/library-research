Why a new library?

Why a New Library?

Peterborough’s Public Library Heritage

The Peterborough Town Library enjoys the distinction of being the first public library in the United States to be supported by tax dollars.

In 1833, when the library was established, Peterborough residents must have recognized that they were creating more than just a building. They were affirming the role of libraries in creating and fostering community, and they understood that a library accessible to the public would be crucial to the educational, cultural, and economic development of the town.

Today, public libraries continue to serve as a cornerstone of our democratic society. Regardless of age, income, ethnic origin, or religion, anyone can access—without charge—information that will enable them to contribute more fully to society, whether as a student, a parent, a worker, or an informed voter.

As our library moves into the 21st century, its basic mission as an institution of lifelong learning remains unchanged. However, the current physical structure limits its ability to provide services and programs that are needed in an ever-changing world. With an improved physical structure, one that is flexible enough to adapt to future needs, the library could enhance the services and programs it offers and serve the community well into the future.

Imagine a library that:

  • Is welcoming to all and is the first place people check to see what’s going on in the community
  • Has space to provide a variety of library experiences at the same time: quiet reading areas, dynamic children activities, after school computer research, and reading group discussions
  • Contains expanded collections of books and DVDs
  • Is fully accessible to all and encourages social networking and lively community interaction among people of all ages and backgrounds
  • Hosts media presentations, including film, video, music, and theater performances
  • Provides meeting space for community organizations and private study rooms
  • Fosters an informed citizenry through increased numbers of public access computers
  • Encourages reading and school readiness for preschoolers
  • Provides expert reference guidance with up to date technological services

Now imagine a library that also uses less energy than the existing building, a building that:

  • Is designed as a whole, integrated system to conserve energy and water and to minimize its impact on our precious environment
  • Provides healthy indoor air quality through the use of non-toxic materials and quiet and efficient ventilation systems
  • Uses renewable energy sources to reduce carbon emissions

 

More information is also available on the 1833 Society website.  The 1833 Society is the nonprofit organization charged with planning and raising funds for a new library.

Why a New Library? 

The aging, substandard condition of much of our library’s physical plant prevents it from being as efficient, effective and welcoming as it could be. These conditions have an impact upon:

 

Safety:  Addressing safety concerns at the library is ongoing, but in some instances repairs and renovations will not provide a remedy.

  • The open stairwell and poor sight lines compromise both staff and patron safety.
  • The McGilvray Historical Room is not accessible to those with disabilities.
  • Librarians on duty cannot maintain visual contact with all who enter and exit; views are blocked on many fronts.
  • Flooring is uneven in places, dirty, and worn.

  • Bathrooms have been enlarged but still are not totally ADA compliant.
  • Other aspects of the library, including both entrances, are not ADA compliant. There are still no fire alarms for the hearing impaired, and there are no sprinklers anywhere.

Operating costs: The current library is expensive to repair, maintain, and run because of poor insulation, cobbled heating and cooling systems, structural flaws, and antiquated fixtures.

  • The existing lighting is poor and inefficient.
  • The existing heating system is an amalgamation of three systems and operates inefficiently. Although the library has a new pellet wood furnace, the heat delivery system needs to be replaced.
  • Temperature cannot be zone controlled. A report by energy consultant Margaret Dillon cited one instance in the original building where the interior brick was heated to a temperature of 104°F, yet three feet away frost had formed on the inside of a window.
  • The existing 1956 and 1978 additions are significantly under-insulated compared to current code. This shortcoming will continue to drain energy dollars as fuel costs rise. The only cost efficient way to get a high-performance envelope is to start over and do it right.

Space usage:  The Library is short on space and is constrained by an inflexible floor plan.

  • According to national standards, our library is too small for the size of our town. The collection space, meeting space, staff work space, children’s area and user seating space are insufficient for a town the size of Peterborough.
  • Insufficient space and antiquated infrastructure limits the ability to incorporate new technology. The space and the electrical outlets to support more public use computers simply aren’t there.
  • Book stacks cannot be relocated because of structural load issues.
  • Although the present Library provides approximately 16,500 square feet of floor space, the usable portion is only about 10,000 square feet, due to the inefficient mechanical and storage areas and the Historical Room’s limited accessibility.
  • The only available meeting space, the basement meeting room, is dark and acoustically unsuitable. An archaic air exchange system provides fresh air, but it draws in untempered air from the outside—dry and cold in the winter, and warm and humid in the summer.
  • Library Services: Building operation costs, space restrictions, and safety issues restrict the library’s ability to fulfill its mission: providing programs and services which support and encourage lifelong learning.
  • Children’s programs: The designated children’s area is not separate from the rest of the library, which limits the scope of programs offered. When kids are kids, other areas are impacted.
  • Reading room function: The area where patrons sit to read newspapers or magazines is not insulated from the traffic of the stairwell or the normal library “noise” of the circulation desk.
  • Book discussion groups, readings, and other small group functions: There are no small, relatively private meeting areas in the library.
  • Technological capabilities: The digital age is upon us. The current infrastructure of the existing library limits its ability to adapt to and support patron requested emerging technologies.

Why can’t we just do a simple renovation, including needed repairs, as opposed to a full renovation of the building?

The Trustees have considered a piece meal approach to making the above improvements and have discovered that it will cost almost as much as a new building. And some renovations are not even possible:

  • Renovation would not provide the high-performance envelope needed to reduce heating and cooling costs.
  • Book stacks would have to remain where they are.
  • Upgrading the structure or making public use of the 6,500 sq. feet of basement storage would not be feasible.

 

Rebuilding the current library will:

  • Support the library’s mission to provide lifelong learning
  • Enhance the library’s ability to offer improved services for all members of the community
  • Provide an opportunity to redefine the library’s relationship within the community
  • Acknowledge the library’s cultural and architectural heritage as it prepares to serve future generations

More information on planning for a Library of the Future can be found on the website of the 1833 Society, the nonprofit organization charged with planning and raising funds for the new library.
